John Cox’s reels form a collection of all the people who loved him. And reel by reel the audience recognizes that love and retrieves it in their own memories. Suddenly, a room that felt empty except for the clicking of an old projector is vibrating with memories of all our pasts.

As technology advances, we lose access to these memories. They are rotting away in outdated formats in our attics, basements, or under our beds. We can save them-at a cost.

The first thing to catch your eye in Katie Rife’s Logan Square apartment is a pyramid of hundreds of Jerry McGuire VHS tapes, stacked on top of one another. Rife collects Jerry McGuires from every thrift store and flea market. To her, they’re a reminder of a slice of history that has disappeared.

“In the heyday of VHS, everyone owned a copy of Jerry McGuire. And it won all the Oscars.” she says. “But now, no one gives a shit about it.” Everyone dumped their VHS copy of it at their local secondhand store.

A lot of people forgot about Jerry McGuire when they didn’t jump to buy the DVD. And when America converted to DVD, blu-ray and digital, it didn’t forget about just Jerry McGuire. Thousands of films fell out of social consciousness.

This is what drives Rife to collect forgotten VHS tapes. She digs through piles at thrift stores, seeking what she calls “hidden gems.” Rife says she had the impulse to start collecting when she realized how many films technology leaves behind. During a transitional period in changing formats, film studios hold the power to keep a work in print, and consequentially, in people’s memories.

Her piles of tapes include treasures like “Mascots 101,” “Michael Bolton’s Winning Softball,” and “Midget Wrestling Bloopers.” She is fascinated with amateur pieces created by people who didn’t have the resources to make Hollywood blockbusters. To her, their films often speak to different groups of people and less to what she calls the “dominant, white, middle-class, aspirational, suburban viewpoint” of Hollywood.

The pieces that end up lost tend to be more raw and immediate looks at unusual lives, Rife says. But she doesn’t just collect tapes made by so-called “marginalized” voices. She also has a giant assortment of what she calls “unfortunate corporate mistakes,” or promotional embarrassments that higher-ups would prefer to sweep under the rug.

“You know, like here,” she says, as she pulls a tape from her shelves that has the five unforgettable members of the Backstreet Boys on the cover. “Here’s ’Back Street Stories.’ A Backstreet Boys fan tape. Like, there’s nothing marginalized about that.”

Rife enjoys both pointing out corporate blunders and giving a wider audience to self-produced amateur pieces. “Both ways you can kinda use for fightin’ the man sort of purposes,” she says.

But to her, VHS is extremely unreliable. “It breaks down like crazy, it doesn’t look good. It’s a formatting nightmare! It’s the worst!” She saves at least six copies of everything in her collection to DVD and digital – so they won’t disappear forever.

Ironically, Rife’s family did not film her as a child, which she thinks might be why she’s so fascinated with other people’s movies. “I just like saving eccentric things,” she says. “Things that make people go, ‘Where the hell did that come from!?’”
